Power Surges Explained: What Happens and Why It Matters

You do not need an electrical engineering degree to understand surge protection. Here is what happens during a power surge in simple terms.

The Basic Concept:

  • Normal household voltage in Sri Lanka: 230V AC
  • Power surge: A sudden, brief increase in voltage above the normal level
  • Duration: Surges typically last microseconds to milliseconds – too fast for you to notice, but long enough to damage electronics
  • Impact: Even small surges (10-20% above normal) can degrade electronics over time; large surges (100%+ above normal) can cause immediate failure

Visual Analogy:

Think of your electrical system like a water pipe:

  • Normal voltage = steady water flow at safe pressure
  • Power surge = sudden pressure spike that can burst pipes or damage fixtures
  • Surge protection = pressure relief valve that safely diverts excess flow

Types of Surges by Magnitude:

Small Surges (10-50V above normal)

Gradual Damage

Cause: Appliance cycling, minor grid fluctuations

Impact: Gradual degradation of electronic components; reduced lifespan of devices

Detection: Often unnoticed until devices fail prematurely

Medium Surges (50-200V above normal)

Immediate Damage

Cause: Load-shedding restoration, transformer switching, nearby lightning

Impact: Immediate damage to sensitive electronics; data loss; component failure

Detection: Devices may stop working, reboot unexpectedly, or show error messages

Large Surges (200V+ above normal)

Catastrophic

Cause: Direct or nearby lightning strike, major grid fault, downed power lines

Impact: Catastrophic damage; fire risk; complete destruction of connected electronics

Detection: Obvious failure; burning smell; visible damage to devices or outlets

Why Colombo Properties Face Elevated Surge Risks

Sri Lanka's electrical environment creates unique surge risks. Understanding these helps you prioritize protection.

Cause 1: Monsoon Thunderstorms and Lightning

High Risk

Why It Happens: Colombo's two monsoon seasons bring frequent thunderstorms; lightning induces massive voltage spikes in power lines

Risk Level: High during May-June and October-November

Protection Gap: Many properties lack adequate lightning protection or grounding

Colombo Context: Coastal properties face higher lightning risk; older buildings may have inadequate grounding systems

Cause 2: Load-Shedding and Grid Transitions

Moderate-High

Why It Happens: When power is restored after load-shedding, voltage can spike as the grid stabilizes; frequent on/off cycling stresses electrical systems

Risk Level: Moderate to high, depending on area and grid stability

Protection Gap: Standard outlets and appliances are not designed for repeated voltage transitions

Colombo Context: Areas with frequent load-shedding experience more surge events; older wiring exacerbates risk

Cause 3: Aging Electrical Infrastructure

High Risk

Why It Happens: Older distribution boards, deteriorating wiring, and corroded connections create resistance points that can generate surges

Risk Level: High in heritage buildings and properties over 20 years old

Protection Gap: Original electrical systems were not designed for modern electronics sensitivity

Colombo Context: Many Colombo homes feature colonial-era wiring; upgrades often focus on capacity, not surge protection

Cause 4: Appliance Cycling and Internal Surges

Moderate

Why It Happens: Large appliances (AC units, refrigerators, water heaters) create small surges when they turn on/off; these travel through shared circuits

Risk Level: Moderate; cumulative effect over time

Protection Gap: Plug-in surge strips protect only the devices plugged into them, not the whole circuit

Colombo Context: High AC usage in tropical climate increases internal surge frequency

Cause 5: Utility Equipment Switching and Faults

Variable

Why It Happens: CEB grid operations, transformer switching, or faults on neighboring properties can send surges through shared infrastructure

Risk Level: Variable; often unpredictable

Protection Gap: Most homeowners have no visibility into grid-side events affecting their property

Colombo Context: Dense urban electrical infrastructure means events on one property can affect neighbors

The Hidden Damage: How Surges Affect Your Devices

Surge damage is not always obvious. Understanding the mechanisms helps you recognize warning signs and prioritize protection.

Immediate Damage (Obvious Failure)

What Happens: Large surges overwhelm electronic components, causing immediate failure

Signs: Device won't turn on; burning smell; visible damage to power supply or circuit board

Affected Devices: Computers, televisions, gaming consoles, smart home hubs, appliances with electronic controls

Recovery: Usually requires repair or replacement; data loss may be permanent

Gradual Degradation (Silent Damage)

What Happens: Repeated small surges stress electronic components, reducing lifespan and performance

Signs: Device becomes less reliable; intermittent errors; slower performance; earlier-than-expected failure

Affected Devices: All electronics, especially those with microprocessors or sensitive circuits

Recovery: Damage is cumulative and irreversible; protection prevents further degradation

Data Corruption and Loss

What Happens: Surges can corrupt data in storage devices or disrupt communication between components

Signs: File corruption; system crashes; network connectivity issues; unexplained reboots

Affected Devices: Computers, NAS devices, smart home systems, security cameras with recording

Recovery: Data recovery may be possible but costly; prevention is far more reliable

Safety Risks Beyond Electronics

What Happens: Severe surges can damage wiring insulation, create fire hazards, or compromise safety systems

Signs: Warm outlets; tripping breakers; unusual smells; flickering lights after surge events

Affected Systems: Entire electrical system, not just connected devices

Recovery: Requires professional electrical inspection and potential rewiring

Warning Signs Your Electronics May Have Suffered Surge Damage:

  • Device fails to power on after a storm or grid event
  • Intermittent operation or unexplained reboots
  • Unusual noises, smells, or heat from power supplies
  • Network devices losing connection or requiring frequent resets
  • Appliances with electronic controls behaving erratically

Surge Protection Options: Which Is Right for Your Colombo Property?

Not all surge protection is equal. Understanding the options helps you choose the right level of protection.

Option 1: Plug-In Surge Strips (Point-of-Use Protection)

Power strips with built-in surge suppression, plugged into standard outlets.

Pros:

  • Low upfront cost (LKR 2,000-8,000 per strip)
  • Easy to install; no electrical work required
  • Good for protecting individual devices or small groups

Cons:

  • Protects only plugged-in devices; not whole circuits
  • Limited surge capacity; may fail during large surges
  • Components degrade over time; replacement often needed every 2-3 years

Best For: Computers, entertainment systems, renters, supplemental protection

Recommended

Option 2: Whole-Home Surge Protection (Service Entrance)

Professional-grade surge protection device installed at your main distribution board.

Pros:

  • Comprehensive protection for entire electrical system
  • Handles large surges that would overwhelm plug-in strips
  • Protects hardwired appliances (AC units, water heaters, security systems)
  • Professional installation ensures proper grounding and compliance
  • Long lifespan (10+ years) with minimal maintenance

Cons:

  • Higher upfront cost (LKR 15,000-35,000 installed)
  • Requires licensed electrician for installation

Best For: Homeowners seeking comprehensive protection, properties with valuable electronics, Colombo homes facing lightning risk

Option 3: Layered Protection Strategy (Recommended)

Combining whole-home protection with point-of-use strips for sensitive devices.

Pros:

  • Maximum protection coverage for all device types
  • Cost-effective balance of comprehensive and targeted protection
  • Redundancy: if one layer fails, others provide backup

Cons:

  • Higher total investment than single-layer approach
  • Requires coordination of multiple protection devices

Best For: Properties with high-value electronics, business-critical equipment, lightning-prone areas

Frequently Asked Questions: Surge Protection in Colombo

Will surge protection protect my electronics during a direct lightning strike?
Whole-home surge protection significantly reduces risk from lightning-induced surges, but no system can guarantee 100% protection against a direct strike. For maximum protection in lightning-prone areas, we recommend layered protection: whole-home device at service entrance, point-of-use strips for sensitive devices, and proper grounding throughout the property. We also advise unplugging highly valuable electronics during severe thunderstorms.
How do I know if my surge protector is still working?
Most quality surge protection devices include indicator lights showing operational status. Whole-home protectors installed by Six7 Electrical include visual indicators and can be tested during maintenance visits. Point-of-use strips often have "protected" indicator lights; if this light goes out, the strip should be replaced. We recommend annual verification of surge protection systems.
Can I install a whole-home surge protector myself?
No. Whole-home surge protection requires installation at the main distribution board, which involves working with live electrical conductors. This work requires licensed electrical expertise to ensure safety, proper grounding, and compliance with Sri Lankan electrical standards. DIY installation risks personal injury, property damage, and voided insurance coverage.
Does surge protection affect my electricity bill or power quality?
No. Properly installed surge protection devices do not consume significant power or affect electricity costs. They remain passive until a surge event occurs, at which point they divert excess voltage to ground. Some advanced models include monitoring features that provide usage data but do not impact power quality or costs.
How often should surge protection devices be replaced?
Whole-home surge protectors typically last 10+ years with proper installation and grounding. Point-of-use strips may need replacement every 2-3 years as internal components degrade with each surge event. We recommend annual inspection of whole-home systems and replacement of point-of-use strips when indicator lights show depletion or after known major surge events.
Will surge protection work during load-shedding or power outages?
Surge protection devices protect against voltage spikes, not power outages. They remain operational during normal grid operation and protect during restoration events when surges commonly occur. For backup power during outages, consider uninterruptible power supply (UPS) systems, which can be integrated with surge protection for comprehensive protection.
Can surge protection prevent fire hazards from electrical faults?
While surge protection primarily protects electronics, properly installed whole-home systems with adequate grounding can reduce fire risk from surge-induced wiring damage. However, surge protection is not a substitute for proper circuit protection (MCBs, RCDs) or regular electrical safety inspections. We recommend comprehensive electrical safety assessments for fire hazard prevention.
