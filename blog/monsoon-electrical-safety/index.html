Common Monsoon Electrical Hazards

Understanding these hazards is the first step to preventing accidents and damage.

Water Ingress into Electrical Components

Rainwater entering outdoor outlets, meter boxes, or wall cavities can cause short circuits, shocks, or fires.

High-Risk Areas: Ground-floor outlets, outdoor lighting, rooftop installations, properties with poor drainage.

Warning Signs: Dampness around outlets, flickering lights after rain, tripping breakers during storms.

Flooding and Submerged Electrical Systems

Standing water from flash floods can reach distribution boards, wiring, or appliances, creating electrocution risks.

High-Risk Areas: Low-lying properties, basement apartments, ground-floor commercial spaces, areas with poor drainage.

Warning Signs: Water pooling near electrical panels, outlets near floor level getting wet, unusual smells after flooding.

Lightning Strikes and Power Surges

Monsoon thunderstorms increase lightning risk, which can send dangerous surges through electrical systems.

High-Risk Areas: Properties with tall structures, rooftop equipment, older wiring without surge protection.

Warning Signs: Lights flickering during storms, appliances malfunctioning after thunder, burnt smell from outlets.

Aging Wiring Exposed to Moisture

Older Colombo homes often have wiring that deteriorates faster when exposed to monsoon humidity and leaks.

High-Risk Areas: Heritage buildings, properties over 20 years old, homes with known wiring issues.

Warning Signs: Frequent breaker tripping during rain, outlets that feel warm, intermittent power loss.

Outdoor Electrical Equipment Damage

Garden lighting, security cameras, and external outlets can fail when exposed to heavy rain and wind.

High-Risk Areas: Properties with extensive outdoor electrical installations, coastal areas with salt-air exposure.

Warning Signs: Outdoor lights not working after rain, visible damage to external fixtures, tripping circuits.

Pre-Monsoon Electrical Safety Checklist

Taking these steps before monsoon season can prevent emergencies and protect your property.

Inspect Outdoor Electrical Components

Action: Check outdoor outlets, lighting, and meter boxes for cracks, corrosion, or loose covers.

Why It Matters: Sealed components prevent water ingress that can cause shorts or shocks.

Test Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters (GFCIs)

Action: Press the "Test" button on GFCI outlets in kitchens, bathrooms, and outdoor areas.

Why It Matters: GFCIs cut power instantly if they detect current leakage, preventing electrocution.

Clear Drainage Around Electrical Infrastructure

Action: Ensure gutters, downspouts, and ground drainage direct water away from meter boxes and outdoor outlets.

Why It Matters: Proper drainage prevents water accumulation near electrical components.

Trim Trees Near Power Lines and Roof Wiring

Action: Cut back branches that could fall on outdoor wiring or service lines during high winds.

Why It Matters: Falling branches can damage wiring, cause outages, or create live-wire hazards.

Install or Verify Surge Protection

Action: Ensure your distribution board has whole-home surge protection, especially for sensitive electronics.

Why It Matters: Surge protectors absorb lightning-induced spikes that can destroy appliances and wiring.

Review Your Emergency Plan

Action: Know the location of your main circuit breaker and ensure all household members can access it.

Why It Matters: Quick power shutdown can prevent fires or electrocution during electrical emergencies.

During Heavy Rain: What to Do (and Not Do)

If you are caught in a monsoon storm, follow these guidelines to minimize electrical risks.

Safety Action 1: Avoid Using Electrical Appliances Near Water

Do: Unplug devices in areas where water might enter (near windows, doors, or low walls).

Do Not: Use phones, laptops, or appliances while standing in water or with wet hands.

Why: Water conducts electricity; even small amounts can create shock hazards.

Safety Action 2: Stay Away from Outdoor Electrical Components

Do: Keep distance from outdoor outlets, lighting, meter boxes, and downed power lines.

Do Not: Attempt to cover or repair outdoor electrical items during active rainfall.

Why: Wet conditions increase electrocution risk; wait for professional help.

Safety Action 3: Monitor Your Electrical System

Do: Watch for flickering lights, unusual smells, or breaker tripping during storms.

Do Not: Ignore warning signs or repeatedly reset tripped breakers without investigation.

Why: These can indicate water ingress or short circuits that require immediate attention.

Safety Action 4: Prepare for Power Outages

Do: Keep flashlights, batteries, and a battery-powered radio accessible.

Do Not: Use candles as primary light sources near electrical panels or wiring.

Why: Candles pose fire risks; battery lights are safer during electrical uncertainty.

Safety Action 5: Know When to Shut Off Power

Do: Turn off the main breaker if you see water near your distribution board or smell burning.

Do Not: Attempt to restore power until a qualified electrician has inspected the system.

Why: Restoring power to a wet or damaged system can cause fires or electrocution.

After the Storm: Inspection and Recovery Steps

Post-storm inspection helps identify hidden damage and prevents future hazards.

Step 1: Visual Inspection (From a Safe Distance)

Look for visible water damage, exposed wiring, or displaced electrical components.

Safety Note: Do not touch anything wet or damaged; assume all components are live until verified safe.

Step 2: Check for Moisture Before Restoring Power

If power was shut off, ensure all electrical components are completely dry before turning power back on.

Safety Note: Use a flashlight, not a phone light, to inspect dark or damp areas.

Step 3: Test Critical Circuits Gradually

After confirming dryness, restore power at the main breaker, then test circuits one by one.

Safety Note: If any breaker trips immediately or you notice unusual behavior, shut off power and call a professional.

Step 4: Inspect Appliances and Electronics

Check appliances that were exposed to moisture or power fluctuations.

Safety Note: Do not plug in devices that were wet or near flooding until verified safe.

Step 5: Schedule a Professional Electrical Inspection

Even if no obvious damage is visible, monsoon exposure can weaken wiring over time.

Benefit: A licensed electrician can identify hidden issues before they become emergencies.

When to Call a Professional Electrician

Do not wait if you notice any of these signs. Electrical hazards can escalate quickly.

Visible Sparks or Arcing

Sparks, flashes, or arcing sounds from outlets, switches, or wiring.

Action: Shut off power and call +94 75 824 4216

Burning Smell or Smoke

Unusual odors or visible smoke from electrical components.

Action: Evacuate, shut off power if safe, call emergency services

Water Contact with Electrical Systems

Flooding reaching outlets, distribution boards, or appliances.

Action: Do not touch, shut off power if safe, call for help

Repeated Breaker Tripping

Circuit breakers that trip frequently during or after monsoon conditions.

Action: Stop using the circuit and schedule professional diagnosis

Unusual Sounds or Behavior

Buzzing, crackling, or humming from electrical panels, outlets, or wiring.

Action: Avoid the area and contact an electrician for assessment

Frequently Asked Questions About Monsoon Electrical Safety

What should I do if my home floods during a monsoon?
First, ensure personal safety: avoid standing water if electrical components may be wet. If safe, shut off power at the main breaker. Do not touch electrical devices or outlets that may be wet. Contact Six7 Electrical at +94 75 824 4216 for emergency assessment once the immediate danger has passed.
How can I tell if my electrical system has water damage?
Warning signs include: frequent breaker tripping, flickering lights, unusual smells (burning or fishy), outlets that feel warm, or visible moisture near electrical components. If you notice any of these after heavy rain, contact a licensed electrician for inspection.
Should I unplug appliances during a thunderstorm?
Yes, for sensitive electronics like computers, televisions, and appliances with microprocessors. Unplugging provides the best protection against lightning-induced power surges. For essential appliances like refrigerators, ensure you have whole-home surge protection installed.
How long should I wait after flooding before restoring power?
Do not restore power until all electrical components are completely dry and have been inspected by a qualified electrician. Moisture can remain hidden in walls or panels; professional assessment ensures safe restoration.
Can I do electrical repairs myself after monsoon damage?
No. Electrical work requires licensed expertise, especially after water exposure. DIY repairs can create hidden hazards, void insurance coverage, and violate Sri Lankan electrical regulations. Always hire a licensed electrician for post-monsoon electrical work.
